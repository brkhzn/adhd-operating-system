# Learning and retention for ADHD cognition

Apply this reference when helping with studying, certifications, note-taking,
knowledge management, reading strategies, skill acquisition, or building
durable knowledge systems.

## The encoding problem

ADHD memory challenges are primarily encoding deficits, not retrieval deficits.
Research shows 75-81% of ADHD cases have central executive working memory
impairments with very large effect sizes. The information never gets properly
stored in the first place. This redirects the entire learning strategy: the
goal is not to review more, but to **encode more deeply** during initial
learning.

University of Haifa research found ADHD adults show adequate within-session
learning but reduced overnight memory consolidation. This deficit was partially
corrected by scheduling training in evening hours, aligning with the ADHD
tendency toward eveningness chronotype. Halving session length also improved
outcomes.

## Spaced repetition with ADHD modifications

Spaced repetition (Anki, RemNote, Mochi) is highly recommended but needs
adaptation to survive ADHD novelty decay.

**Session length:** Cap at 15-20 minutes. Research shows halving session length
benefits ADHD learners. Short daily sessions beat long irregular ones.

**Algorithm:** Use FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler) over the legacy SM-2
algorithm. FSRS uses machine learning to adapt to individual memory patterns,
handling irregular review schedules more gracefully, which is critical since
ADHD users will inevitably miss days.

**Card design:** Atomic cards only. One concept per card. Keep answers to one
sentence or a single visual. Use image occlusion for diagrams. Visual mnemonics
compensate for rote memory weakness.

**Target retention:** Set around 85-90%. Higher targets increase review burden
and trigger overwhelm. Lower targets let too much slip.

**Habit integration:** Anchor Anki reviews to an existing daily habit (after
morning coffee, during commute, before bed). The most common ADHD failure mode
is intense use for two weeks, then abandonment, then guilt over 600+ overdue
cards. When this happens, use Anki's "reschedule" feature to reset rather than
attempting to fight through the backlog. Restarting fresh is better than
quitting permanently.

**The abandon-and-restart protocol:** When the user reports an abandoned Anki
deck, do not suggest "just do 10 cards a day to catch up." The Wall of Awful
around an overdue deck is usually insurmountable. Instead: export the deck,
delete it, reimport as a fresh deck with rescheduled intervals, and start
with a 5-minute daily commitment.

## Active recall methods

Since the core problem is encoding depth, active recall forces deeper
processing than passive review. These methods are ranked by effectiveness
for ADHD cognition:

### Feynman Technique (ADHD-modified)

Choose a concept. Explain it aloud in plain terms as if teaching someone who
knows nothing about it. Identify where the explanation breaks down. Return
to source material for those gaps. Simplify and explain again.

The ADHD modification: **do this verbally, not in writing.** Explaining
concepts while walking adds physical movement (which upregulates dopamine
and norepinephrine), engages auditory processing, and is far less
friction than sitting down to write an explanation. Use voice memos to
capture the explanation if it's worth keeping.

### Practice testing

The single most effective study method by research consensus. Tests provide
immediate feedback (dopamine reward), create time pressure (urgency
activation), and reveal specific gaps (targeted re-study rather than
unfocused review).

For certification study: use official practice exams, question banks, and
apps. Read every answer choice twice, because ADHD impulsivity causes
jumping to the first right-sounding option. Track which question types
are consistently missed and focus review there.

### Teach-back

Explain material to another person (or to a voice recorder, or to Claude).
The social accountability of teaching activates ADHD brains that don't
activate for solo study. Teaching also reveals comprehension gaps that
passive confidence masks.

### Interleaving

Mix topics within a study session rather than blocking one topic per session.
Counterintuitive, but research shows interleaved practice produces better
long-term retention. For ADHD, it also provides the micro-novelty of
switching between subjects, fighting the attention fade that sets in with
sustained single-topic focus.

## Note-taking systems

### The Zettelkasten method

Atomic, linked notes align remarkably well with ADHD cognition. One idea
per note reduces cognitive load. Non-linear linking mirrors associative
ADHD thinking. Following links provides novelty-seeking reward. The system
organizes itself through connections rather than requiring rigid hierarchical
structure, which would demand the executive function ADHD impairs.

**Obsidian** is the top tool recommendation. Graph-based linking makes
patterns visible. Key community plugins: Spaced Repetition (flashcards from
notes), Dataview (queries across notes), Templater (reduce capture friction),
Calendar, Excalidraw (visual notes and mind maps).

Bryan Jenks' integrated workflow: fleeting notes (quick captures) flow into
literature notes (processed from a source), then into permanent notes
(evergreen ideas in your own words), with Anki flashcards automatically
generated from key insights.

### PARA method (Tiago Forte)

Organizational backbone for any knowledge system: Projects (active,
time-bounded), Areas (ongoing responsibilities), Resources (reference
material), Archives (completed/inactive). The key ADHD benefit: every
piece of information has exactly one place to go, eliminating the "where
does this go?" decision paralysis.

**Progressive Summarization** works in layers: highlight source material,
then bold the highlights, then write a brief summary. Each layer takes
one pass. This matches the ADHD brain's preference for working in short
bursts rather than processing everything in one deep session.

### Critical principle: work IN the system, not ON the system

The most dangerous ADHD trap with any knowledge management tool is spending
weeks perfecting templates, plugins, and organizational structure in a
hyperfocus trance instead of actually taking notes. One note, one idea,
one link. Build from there. The system's value comes from content, not
from architecture. When the user starts talking about optimizing their
Obsidian setup, gently redirect toward actually using it.

## Managing hyperfocus for learning

Hyperfocus is triggered by novelty, competition/challenge, personal interest,
pressure, and playfulness. It can be deliberately aimed at learning tasks
by creating these conditions:

**Schedule learning during natural hyperfocus windows.** Track when deep
focus naturally occurs (often mid-to-late morning or late evening) and
protect those windows for the hardest material.

**Environmental triggers.** Specific music, specific headphones, specific
location signal "learning mode." Classical conditioning bypasses executive
function resistance.

**Set a 90-minute alarm.** Hyperfocus can consume 4+ hours and cause
multi-day recovery crashes. The alarm creates a checkpoint to assess whether
continued focus is intentional or compulsive.

**The Flowtime Technique** (alternative to rigid Pomodoro): work until
natural attention wanes, note the duration, take a proportional break.
This respects the brain's natural rhythm rather than imposing external
interruptions.

## Reading strategies

ADHD and sustained reading conflict directly. The strongest strategies:

**Audiobook plus physical text simultaneously.** Following along with text
while listening doubles sensory input, prevents mind-wandering through the
narrator's pacing, and creates stronger memory traces through dual encoding.
Speed adjustment to 1.25x-1.5x often helps.

**Active annotation.** Read with a pen, highlighter, or digital annotation
tool. The physical act of marking text creates engagement anchors. Marginalia
in your own words forces processing.

**Strategic chapter selection.** Never suggest reading a technical book
front-to-back. Use practice tests or table of contents to identify knowledge
gaps, then read targeted chapters. Random access to relevant material
beats forced linear progression.

**The 10-page contract.** Commit to 10 pages. After 10, decide whether to
continue. Lowering the commitment reduces the initiation barrier. Momentum
usually carries past the minimum.

## Brown's executive function model applied to learning

Thomas Brown's six clusters map directly to learning failure points:

**Activation** (getting started): use implementation intentions ("if it's 7pm,
then I open Anki"), body doubling, environmental cues, and the 2-minute rule
for starting study sessions.

**Focus** (sustaining attention): Pomodoro timers, active annotation,
multimodal learning (audio + visual + kinesthetic), and environmental
control (noise-canceling headphones, website blockers).

**Effort** (maintaining pace): break study into 25-45 minute blocks,
alternate between harder and easier material within sessions, and schedule
breaks before fatigue hits rather than after.

**Emotion** (managing frustration): reframe difficulty as "desirable
difficulty" (a well-established learning science concept), celebrate small
completions, and treat confusion as useful signal rather than evidence of
inability.

**Memory** (retention): this is where the entire PKM system, spaced
repetition, and active recall toolkit operates. Externalize everything.
Trust no thought to memory alone.

**Action** (execution under pressure): for exams, use deliberate slow-reading
of questions, systematic elimination of wrong answers, and time-checking
checkpoints. For practical application, use checklists and runbooks.

Brown's critical finding: high-IQ adults with ADHD still show significant
executive function impairments. Intelligence does not protect against
EF-mediated learning challenges. The systems are not crutches.

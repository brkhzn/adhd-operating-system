# Project management for ADHD cognition

Apply this reference when helping with tasks, projects, deadlines, productivity
systems, creative project management, or anything involving planning and
execution over time.

## External scaffolding is the whole game

Barkley's core treatment principle: since internal executive functions are
impaired, external systems must replace them. Three things to externalize:

**Information** at the point of performance. If a task isn't visible where
work happens, it won't get done. Whiteboards, dashboards, sticky notes on
monitors, and always-open task lists beat buried Notion databases.

**Motivation** through artificial structure. Body doubling, accountability
partners, streaks with no shame on breaks, visible progress markers, and
completion celebrations. Internal motivation cannot be relied upon for
non-novel tasks.

**Time** through physical representation. Visual timers, analog clocks,
calendar blocking with buffer, and explicit "this will take X minutes"
annotations on every task. The ADHD brain perceives time as binary (now
and not-now), so every time-dependent action needs an external trigger.

## Time management approaches that survive ADHD

### What fails

Rigid time-blocking (creates avoidance when a block is missed). Strict
Pomodoro (25 minutes is arbitrary and interrupting flow state is costly).
Detailed hourly schedules (one disruption cascades into full-day failure
and shame). Commitment to "catching up" after a bad day (compounds the
problem).

### What works

**Energy-based scheduling.** Map tasks to cognitive energy, not clock time.
Deep analytical work during peak brain hours. Routine administration during
troughs. Most ADHD adults peak mid-to-late morning (30-90 minutes after
stimulant onset, if applicable).

**Time-boxing over time-blocking.** Set a fixed time to spend on a task
regardless of completion. "I will work on X for 40 minutes" removes the
pressure of finishing and lowers the initiation barrier.

**Modified Pomodoro (25-45 minute flexible intervals).** Let the interval
length flex based on the task and current focus. Use the timer as an
attention recall mechanism, not a rigid structure. If focus is flowing at
25 minutes, extend to 45.

**Theme days or theme blocks.** Reduce context-switching cost by dedicating
days or half-days to a single domain: infrastructure Monday, security Tuesday,
writing Wednesday evening. The brain doesn't have to re-initialize for each task.

**The 2-minute rule.** Any task under 2 minutes gets done immediately upon
identification. Prevents the accumulation of tiny tasks that create overwhelming
backlogs.

**Double-and-buffer.** Double all time estimates, add 25% buffer between blocks.
When a block is missed, apply the "next available slot" rule. Never compress.
The system must forgive gracefully.

## Tool evaluation criteria

When the user asks about task managers, project tools, or productivity apps,
evaluate against these ADHD-specific criteria:

1. **Capture friction.** Can you add a task in under 5 seconds without navigating
   menus or choosing a project? If capture is hard, tasks go uncaptured.

2. **Today view.** Can you see ONLY today's tasks with the backlog hidden? The
   full list creates paralysis. Today's list creates focus.

3. **Motivational design.** Does completing tasks feel rewarding? Animations,
   progress bars, streaks, mascots. These aren't frivolous; they provide the
   external dopamine the brain doesn't generate internally.

4. **Graceful degradation.** When the user doesn't open the app for five days,
   what happens? Do 47 overdue tasks glare in red? Or does the system gently
   resurface what matters? Red-shame apps get abandoned.

5. **Workflow flexibility.** Can the user switch from Kanban to list to calendar
   without migrating data? Novelty decay kills rigid tools. The framework
   should stay; the view should be swappable.

6. **Integration depth.** Does it pull from existing work tools (email, Slack,
   Jira, calendar) or require manual duplication? Every manual entry is a
   friction point that compounds over time.

### Top recommendations by profile

**Flexible daily driver:** Amazing Marvin. 50+ togglable strategy modules,
procrastination wizard, random task picker, flexible views. Best for users
who need to change their workflow without changing their tool.

**Work integration layer:** Sunsama. Pulls tasks from Jira, Asana, Gmail, Slack,
GitHub into a guided daily planning session. Daily shutdown ritual built in.
Best for users in corporate environments with existing PM tools.

**All-in-one simplicity:** TickTick. Tasks, calendar, habit tracker, Pomodoro
timer in one app. Lower learning curve than Marvin.

**Lowest capture friction:** Todoist. Natural language input, keyboard shortcuts,
fast mobile app. Best for users who need to capture fast and sort later.

**Enterprise tools (Jira, Asana, Linear)** are typically poor for solo ADHD
users. They're designed for team coordination, not individual executive function
support. No motivational design, overwhelming visibility, high administrative
overhead. If required by the workplace, layer a personal tool (Sunsama, Marvin)
on top.

## Task paralysis: the mechanism and the toolkit

Task paralysis is executive dysfunction plus emotional dysregulation triggering
a freeze response. The brain cannot plan the first step AND manage the dread
simultaneously, so it does neither.

### The Wall of Awful (Brendan Mahan)

Every past failure, shame experience, and disappointment adds a brick to an
emotional wall between the person and task initiation. Strategies map to five
responses:

- Staring at the wall (frozen rumination): interrupt the loop physically.
  Stand up, walk to a different room, drink cold water, change sensory input.
- Going around it (avoidance): identify whether "productive procrastination"
  is actually useful. Sometimes the avoidance task matters too.
- Hulk-smashing through (anger-fueled burst): works short-term, builds a
  bigger wall long-term. Not sustainable.
- **Putting a door in it**: change the environment. Body double. Shrink the task.
  Change location. Play music. Remove friction. This is the sustainable path.
- Climbing it: awareness plus acceptance plus deliberate action. Takes practice
  and often benefits from coaching or CBT.

### Paralysis breakers (tactical)

- Shrink to absurdity: "I don't have to finish the report, I just have to
  open the document and type one sentence."
- Random selection: use a task picker app or literally pull a task from a hat
  when choice itself is the barrier.
- Body doubling: work alongside another person (virtual or physical).
- Movement first: 5 minutes of walking before attempting the task. Physical
  movement upregulates dopamine and norepinephrine.
- Reverse Eat the Frog: start with the easiest, most appealing task to build
  momentum. The standard "hardest thing first" advice often backfires for ADHD.
- Externalized commitment: tell someone you'll have X done by Y. Artificial
  urgency activates the ADHD brain when importance alone cannot.

## Multi-step project management

### The novelty curve

Every project follows the same arc for ADHD: thrilling start, painful middle,
rushed or abandoned end. The middle dies because novelty is gone, the reward
is distant, and the brain perceives the remaining work as an undifferentiated
mass.

Counter this with: visible progress tracking (Kanban boards, progress bars,
chapter completion percentages), artificial milestones with micro-rewards every
3-5 days, regular novelty injections (change where you work on it, change the
music, change the order of subtasks), external accountability at the cadence
of your attention span (weekly check-ins for long projects, daily for critical
ones), and parallel "dessert projects" that provide completion dopamine without
derailing the main effort.

### Creative project management

Writing, art, design, and other creative projects share specific ADHD challenges:
the work is self-directed (no external deadlines), progress is subjective
(hard to see "done"), and the scope is ambiguous (the project grows in the mind).

Structural countermeasures: outline extensively before writing (detailed
scene-by-scene for fiction, section-by-section for nonfiction) because the
brain needs to see the whole shape to know where it is. Set small daily output
goals (250-500 words, one scene, one sketch) rather than ambitious ones that
create shame. Use tools that support non-linear work (Scrivener for writing,
Figma for design) because ADHD brains don't process linearly. Create external
deadlines through communities, partners, or paid accountability. Finish small
adjacent projects regularly (short stories, one-page designs) to generate
completion dopamine.

### Context-switching tax

Research shows task-switching costs up to 40% of productive time, and ADHD
individuals show substantially larger switch costs, especially under high
working memory load.

Minimize switches by batching similar-context tasks. Leave explicit
"breadcrumbs" before switching (a one-sentence note about exactly where
you are and what comes next). Use separate tools for separate domains to
leverage environmental cues. Never mix project contexts in the same physical
or digital workspace.

## Body doubling

Working alongside another person to maintain focus. Limited formal research
but strong community evidence and a 2024 ACM study showing most neurodivergent
participants used the technique "long before learning its name."

**Virtual platforms:** Focusmate (1:1 video pairing, 25/50/75 min sessions,
free tier with 3/week), Flow Club (small-group facilitated, ADHD-specific
community), Writers' Hour (for creative work specifically).

**In-office:** the office environment itself provides natural body doubling.
For solo workers, even a coffee shop or library provides sufficient ambient
social presence to activate performance.

**AI body doubling:** a 2025 VR study showed participants finished tasks
faster with AI body doubles compared to working alone. Tools like Focusmate
are exploring AI-assisted sessions.

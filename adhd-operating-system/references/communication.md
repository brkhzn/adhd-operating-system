# Communication for ADHD cognition

Apply this reference when helping with meetings, email, Slack, workplace
interactions, disclosure decisions, interpersonal dynamics, rejection
sensitivity, or masking-related topics.

## Attention drift in conversation

The ADHD prefrontal cortex draws attention toward internal thoughts rather
than external stimuli. Working memory limitations make it hard to hold
conversational information while simultaneously processing meaning. The
brain focuses on connections between things rather than sequential content,
causing drift into associated thought chains.

### Recovery techniques

**Confident Repeat Request.** Reframe the moment: "I got caught up thinking
about what you were saying and need you to repeat that last part. I don't
want to miss anything." Delivered with confidence, this reads as engagement,
not inattention.

**Sub-vocal repetition.** Silently repeat the speaker's key words as they
talk. This occupies the verbal working memory channel and prevents it from
being hijacked by internal thoughts.

**Notebook Capture Method.** Write down intrusive thoughts in a dedicated
"parking lot" section, then return to listening. The act of externalizing
the thought relieves the working memory pressure to hold it.

**Curiosity anchoring.** Fill the gap between your processing speed and the
speaker's pace by generating genuine questions about where the conversation
is heading. This engages novelty-seeking productively.

**Physical engagement.** Pen in hand (taking notes or doodling), fidget tool,
or postural adjustments. Low-level physical activity maintains arousal without
distracting others.

## Email management

Email is ADHD kryptonite. Each incoming message delivers a small dopamine
hit, making inbox-checking compulsive. The "I'll reply later" trap creates
mounting paralysis until the backlog becomes an emotional wall.

### System design

**Scheduled processing windows.** Two to three dedicated email blocks per day.
Between blocks, scan only for VIP senders (cap at 3-5 people). Close the
email client entirely between blocks. If the workplace culture expects faster
response, communicate the schedule explicitly.

**Three-category visual system.** Red/star for action required, yellow/snooze
for waiting on response, everything else is read-and-archive. The inbox should
contain only items requiring action. Everything else moves out.

**Template library.** Pre-write templates for recurring situations: acknowledgment
replies, meeting follow-ups, status updates, vendor communications, deadline
requests. Lower the composition barrier to near-zero for routine messages.

**The two-minute email rule.** If a reply takes under two minutes, send it
immediately during a processing window. Never flag it for later. The tiny
tasks compound into an impenetrable backlog faster than any other category.

**Boundary-setting signature.** "I process email at [times]. For urgent matters,
reach me at [alternate channel]." This sets expectations without requiring
repeated individual explanations.

### Tool recommendations

**SaneBox:** AI-sorted inbox priority. Automatically filters unimportant email
out of the main view. Particularly useful for high-volume inboxes.

**Superhuman:** Speed-focused email client with keyboard shortcuts, "remind me
later" scheduling, and read-status indicators. High price ($30/month) but
the speed advantage is meaningful for email-anxious users.

**Snooze features (native in Gmail, Outlook):** Send emails forward in time
to when they're actionable. Clears the inbox without losing the task.

## Slack and real-time messaging

The ADHD professional community consistently identifies Slack as one of the
most disruptive tools in the modern workplace. The constant notification
stream, channel proliferation, and expectation of rapid response create a
perfect storm for attention fragmentation.

### System design

**Close Slack during deep work.** Not minimize; close. The temptation to
"just check" is too strong with a minimized window.

**Notification discipline.** Limit notifications to DMs and mentions in 2-3
critical channels. Mute everything else. Set notification hours to core
business hours only.

**Status as boundary communication.** Use Slack status proactively: "Focusing,
slow to reply" or "Deep work until 2pm." This communicates availability
without requiring explanation.

**Batch checking.** Check Slack two to three times daily at scheduled intervals.
Skim channel headers first, then go deep on anything requiring response.

**Thread discipline.** Always reply in threads, never in the main channel. This
reduces the cognitive cost of scanning channels for relevant content.

## Meeting strategies

Meetings are an executive function gauntlet: sustained attention, working
memory (tracking the discussion), inhibitory control (not interrupting), and
task switching (moving between meeting topics) are all demanded simultaneously.

### Before the meeting

**Five-minute prep.** Review the agenda, write one to two questions you want
answered, identify your role and expected contribution. This primes the
brain with relevant schemas that make following the discussion easier.

**Review materials in advance.** If materials are distributed before the meeting,
review them during a medication-effective window. Processing new information
in real time while also tracking a discussion exceeds ADHD working memory
capacity.

**Request agendas.** If meetings don't have agendas, request them. Frame it
as a productivity suggestion for the whole team, not a personal accommodation.
"I find we get more done when we have a clear agenda. Would it help to send
one in advance?"

### During the meeting

**Volunteer to take visible notes.** Whiteboard, shared doc, or screen-shared
notes. This creates engagement through active participation, provides
micro-breaks, and positions you as contributing rather than drifting.

**Thoughts-to-Share notebook.** Capture ideas as they arise in a dedicated
space. This reduces the interrupt impulse because you won't fear losing the
thought. Share relevant items during natural pauses.

**AI transcription.** Otter.ai, Supernormal, Fireflies, or built-in Teams/Zoom
transcription. This frees you from frantic note-taking and provides a
searchable record. For important meetings, review the transcript afterward
to catch anything attention missed.

**Physical engagement.** Pen in hand, doodling on paper, fidget tool under the
table. If on video, keep camera on (accountability) but use speaker view
(reduces visual distraction from the gallery).

**Triangle breathing between back-to-back meetings.** Inhale 4 counts, hold 4,
exhale 4. Resets the nervous system for the context switch. Even 60 seconds
between meetings helps.

### After the meeting

**Immediate capture.** Within 5 minutes of the meeting ending, write down
your action items and any commitments you made. Working memory degrades
rapidly. If the meeting had no clear action items for you, write "no actions"
to create closure.

### Structural advocacy

At a senior level, advocate for: shorter meetings (15-minute stand-ups over
60-minute sit-downs), clear time-bounded agenda items, high-priority items
placed early, meeting-free blocks on team calendars, and the elimination of
meetings that could have been emails or Slack threads.

## Disclosure and self-advocacy

### The strategic approach

ADHD is recognized as a disability under the ADA. Disclosure is optional and
should be treated as a strategic decision, not a moral obligation.

**Phase 1: Self-accommodation.** Implement personal strategies independently.
Noise-canceling headphones, task management tools, email processing schedules,
meeting prep routines. Many effective accommodations require no disclosure.

**Phase 2: Strengths-based framing.** Request changes using productivity
language, not diagnostic language. Examples:

- Noise: "I'm most effective in quieter environments. Is there a space I
  could use for focused work?"
- Meeting format: "I contribute better when I can review materials in advance.
  Could we send agendas 24 hours before?"
- Written follow-ups: "I want to capture everything accurately. Could you
  email me the key action items?"
- Headphones: "I'm most productive with background noise reduction during
  focused tasks."
- Flexible scheduling: "I do my best analytical work in the morning. Could
  we schedule my deep-work blocks before noon?"

Each frames the request around outcomes rather than diagnosis.

**Phase 3: Formal disclosure.** Consider only if self-accommodation and
strengths-based framing are insufficient. Go through HR, not your direct
manager, for legal protection. Request specific accommodations (not a
general "I have ADHD"), provide documentation from a treating clinician,
and frame accommodations as tools that improve your contribution.

### Risks of disclosure

Research shows that even high-performing employees sometimes face changed
perceptions after disclosing. In smaller organizations or less progressive
cultures, disclosure may change how your work is evaluated. The decision
is personal, context-dependent, and reversible only in theory. Disclose
based on specific accommodation needs, not general transparency impulses.

## Rejection sensitivity dysphoria

An intense emotional response to real or perceived rejection, acknowledged
by 98-99% of ADHD adults. Around one-third report it as the most impairing
aspect of their condition. It is brain-based and innate, experienced as
physically painful, and disproportionate to the actual situation.

### How it shows up at work

- Avoidance: not speaking in meetings, not offering ideas, not applying
  for opportunities
- People-pleasing: overpromising to prevent any possibility of criticism
- Perfectionism as armor: working to be above reproach rather than working
  to be effective
- Misinterpreting feedback: constructive criticism perceived as personal attack
- Rumination: hours or days replaying a brief interaction

### Management strategies

**Request feedback in writing.** This provides processing time and prevents
the emotional hijack that occurs with unexpected verbal criticism.

**Cognitive pause.** When triggered, count to five before responding. The
initial emotional spike is neurological and will diminish within seconds.
The goal is to prevent the spike from driving the response.

**Reality testing.** "Is this objectively happening, or am I interpreting?"
Write down the actual words said versus the emotional narrative. The gap
is usually enormous.

**Separate self-worth from work output.** Easier said than done, but
journaling that distinguishes "I made an error in this specific deliverable"
from "I am bad at my job" builds the neural pathway over time.

**CBT techniques.** The Safren Model CBT for adult ADHD specifically addresses
cognitive distortions around rejection. A therapist trained in adult ADHD
can provide structured tools.

## Masking fatigue

Nearly 70% of adults with ADHD hide symptoms at work. Those who engage in
high compensatory behaviors report 2.3 times higher mental exhaustion.

The masking-burnout cycle: compensation phase (elaborate workarounds, extra
effort) leads to diminishing returns (increasing effort, declining output),
then symptom intensification (core symptoms worsen as compensatory capacity
depletes), then functional collapse.

### Strategic unmasking

Identify which coping behaviors are genuinely helpful systems versus purely
performative masking. Keep the systems; release the performances.

**Energy budgeting.** Treat masking as an energy expenditure. Schedule
high-masking activities (leadership presentations, client meetings)
with recovery time afterward. Never schedule back-to-back high-masking events.

**Gradual authenticity.** Start small: use a fidget tool openly, decline a
meeting that doesn't need you, say "let me think about that and get back
to you" instead of forcing an immediate answer.

**Identify safe contexts.** Some environments tolerate authenticity better
than others. Close team members, trusted managers, and informal settings
are testing grounds for reduced masking.

## When the user asks Claude to help draft messages

Apply ADHD communication awareness to message drafting:

- Keep messages short. The user's ADHD-shaped communication style may
  naturally tend toward long, tangential messages. Help them trim to
  the essential point plus one supporting detail.
- Lead with the ask or the action item. Context comes after.
- Use the message compose tool with multiple variants when the situation
  involves competing goals (being direct versus being diplomatic, etc.).
- Flag if a drafted message might be over-explaining or defensive (common
  RSD-driven patterns) and offer a tighter version.
